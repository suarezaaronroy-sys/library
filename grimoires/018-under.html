Grimoire 018 · under

Under.

An hour I mostly wasn't there for — and can't stop returning to.

Not a manual. Not a field note. An attempt to hold a feeling still — in words and in colour — before it faded, because the parts that fade are usually the parts worth keeping.

I · the descent

There is no line you feel yourself cross.

I don't remember the moment it took. One second the room was the room. Then, with no transition and no sound of a door closing, the room stopped assembling itself the way rooms do. Nobody warns you that consciousness has a resolution — and that it can be turned down.

the world, losing its detail
zoomed
II · everything zoomed in

The world stopped being made of things.

Everything was zoomed in. Not magnified — zoomed, the way a photo goes when you push past its last real detail and fall into the grain. The world stopped being made of things and started being made of pixels, and I was somewhere down inside them.

The sound came with it: slowed, then simplified, then garbled — not because anything was broken, but because I was too close to it to hear the whole of it. Vision did the same. It flattened. It gave up its depth. Everything arrived one layer thick.

III · one pixel at a time

I was a single pixel, and the only thing that happened was that I moved.

Jumping from one colour to the next. One square of nothing to another. That was the entire content of the hour: colour, then a different colour. I had no body I could find. No edges. One dimension, if that.

And here is the part I keep turning over — my thoughts were completely intact. I was still me, still thinking clearly, still narrating. I just couldn't zoom out. I had the mind and none of the map. I was microscopic, genuinely and not as a figure of speech: like an ant on a four-lane highway, aware of every grain of the asphalt beneath it, with no way to understand that it was standing on a road.

IV · the abstract hour

Sensation without a world.

Later I understood what had happened. Normally, experience is of something — it points at a world. Every colour belongs to an object, every sound to a source, composed so fast and so automatically that you mistake the finished picture for reality itself. Under, the assembly line stopped. I was left holding the raw material: colour and sound and dimension with nothing they belonged to. Sensation without a referent. The pixels before they resolve into an image.

It was abstract in the truest sense — not a painting of anything, just form and quality and movement, closer to a colour field than a photograph. I spend my life making abstractions: models, maps, systems, this whole library. But I didn't make this one. The world got abstract on its own, and I was only there for it. The abstracter became the abstraction.

colour with nothing it belonged to
V · too small to be responsible

It should have been frightening. It wasn't.

It was liberating. For someone who carries the whole system in his head every waking hour — always holding the overview, always responsible for seeing how the parts fit — being reduced to a single pixel with nothing to integrate was the closest thing to rest I have felt in a long time.

No whole to maintain. No structure to keep aloft. Just the one colour at a time, and no obligation to make it mean anything. There is a freedom, it turns out, in being too small to be responsible for the whole.

A freedom in being too small to be responsible for the whole.

VI · the pressure release

A valve, opening.

Surreal, and simple, and clean. Not a high — nothing I'd chase. Just pressure, released. The anesthesia didn't give me a new feeling so much as it briefly removed an old one, and only then did I notice how much I'd been carrying the entire time. You don't feel the weight until it's set down.

VII · zoomed back out

Coming back is not dramatic either.

The resolution turns up. The pixels resolve. The room reassembles into a room, depth returns, sound gathers itself back into meaning, and you are the ordinary size of a person again, holding the ordinary weight of a life. It happens so smoothly you'd never know the parts had ever come apart.

But I know now. I have seen underneath. For a little while I was too small to see the whole — and it turned out the whole was the heaviest thing I own.

Held still soon after, before it faded. Some things are worth writing down precisely because they refuse to stay.
