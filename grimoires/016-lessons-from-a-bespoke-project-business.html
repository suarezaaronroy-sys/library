Doctrine

The Job Is Not Sold Until It Can Be Fulfilled

A bespoke project business is not a simple payment-and-delivery operation. It is a measurement, specification, procurement or manufacture, installation, service, and reconciliation workflow.

The core operating risk is false certainty. A customer can accept a quote, a deposit can be paid, and a job can still be unsafe to order because the final measurement, specification, supplier lead time, or installation readiness is not locked.

Operating law: payment unlocks the next controlled stage. Payment does not prove the job is manufacture-ready.

The full lifecycle

  1. Customer enquiry arrives. Capture identity, contact route, address, work type, photos, measurements, and urgency.
  2. Office triages the route. Decide whether to ask for more information, give a rough estimate, book a survey, or decline/redirect.
  3. Survey or estimate path begins. Rough info can support an estimate; site uncertainty pushes the job to survey.
  4. Surveyor confirms technical detail. Measurements, constraints, product type, access, and feasibility become quote inputs.
  5. Quote is issued and versioned. The exact quote version matters because later changes become variations.
  6. Quote is followed up. The office keeps the decision loop moving without harassing the customer.
  7. Customer accepts. Written acceptance triggers sold/contract setup, not supplier ordering.
  8. Contract and deposit invoice are created. CRM/job record, invoice references, payment links, and bank-transfer references must align.
  9. Payment is matched. Card, bank, or payment-provider activity is reconciled to the correct invoice and contract.
  10. Final measurement locks the specification. Exact dimensions, finish, materials, components, access, and install details are confirmed.
  11. Pre-order control clears variations. Scope changes, price changes, customer-supplied items, and split invoices are resolved.
  12. Supplier order is placed. Order acknowledgement, supplier reference, delivery expectation, and documents are saved.
  13. Delivery is checked. Materials are matched against the order; missing or damaged items are escalated.
  14. Installation is scheduled and confirmed. Product confidence, installer availability, customer access, and prep instructions align.
  15. Work is completed or snags are logged. Completion photos/notes and remedial items feed final finance decisions.
  16. Final balance is invoiced and reconciled. Credits, refunds, extras, and payment references must match the contract.
  17. Warranty, review, and closure are handled. The job closes only when support obligations and CRM status are clear.

Pipeline is not status

The pipeline answers where the job is. Status answers what is currently blocking, advancing, or complicating it. These are separate truths. Mixing them is how a stage field becomes a junk drawer.

Status / Tags

Many simultaneous conditions: awaiting customer, awaiting supplier, deposit paid, delivery issue, remedial open, finance query, review not requested.

Roles Are Control Points

The process breaks when everything is "with the office" or "with the team." The process holds when every decision has an owner.

Admin / Office

Flow controller

Inbox triage, lead capture, appointments, quote follow-up, customer updates, payment chasing, job notes, and handoff discipline.

Survey / Technical

Feasibility owner

Measurements, product choices, site constraints, final measure, remedial diagnosis, and whether a job is safe to order.

Accounts

Money truth owner

Invoice references, payment matching, supplier statements, missing invoices, refunds, credits, and reconciliation evidence.

Installer / Job Owner

Delivery truth owner

On-site completion, access issues, missing parts, damage, customer readiness, snags, photos, and remedial needs.

The Pipeline

Ten Stages, Ten Gates

A job moves forward only when the gate at the end of its current stage is true. If the gate is not true, the job needs a next action, not optimism.

Stage 1 - LeadNew enquiry to qualified route

Lead intake captures enough information to decide the route. The aim is not to quote instantly; the aim is to avoid losing, duplicating, or misclassifying the job.

  • Customer name, phone, email, and site address are captured or requested.
  • Work type is broadly identified: installation, replacement, repair, service, warranty, commercial, landlord, or other.
  • Photos and rough measurements are requested where they help estimate.
  • Urgency, safety, security, and repeat-customer status are known.
  • Lead is created or located in the CRM.
Gate: move forward only when the customer route is clear: estimate, survey, technical review, remedial triage, or polite decline.
Stage 2 - Survey / EstimateInformation to price responsibly

The estimate path is for rough qualification. The survey path is for uncertainty, complexity, or customer preference. Both paths must preserve the measurement basis.

RouteUse whenControl
EstimateCustomer can provide useful photos, rough width/height, and enough context for a non-final price.State that price may change after survey/final measure.
SurveyScope is complex, measurements are unreliable, access matters, or customer wants a visit.Confirm surveyor, date/time, address, and CRM appointment.
Technical reviewRepair feasibility, unusual materials, structural/access constraints, warranty/remedial issue.Assign a technical owner before promising outcome.

Measurement confidence ladder

LevelMeaningAllowed decision
Customer rough dimensionsUseful for qualification only. Customer-provided sizes may be incomplete or inaccurate.Rough estimate, photo request, survey recommendation.
Estimate dimensionsOffice or estimator has enough basis for a non-final price.Estimate with caveat; do not manufacture.
Survey dimensionsSite visit or technical review supports a proper quote.Quote, revise quote, or request final measure.
Final manufacture dimensionsExact order-ready dimensions and specification are locked.Supplier order may proceed after finance and variation checks.
Gate: move forward only when enough information exists to quote responsibly, the measurement confidence level is known, and any uncertainty is logged where the next person can find it.
Stage 3 - QuotePriced offer to written acceptance

The quote is the priced commitment. It must preserve scope, version, terms, expiry/validity, and measurement basis. A changed quote is not an edit in the air; it is a versioned business event.

  • Correct customer and address.
  • Correct product, location, material, finish, components, accessories, and install inclusion.
  • Measurement basis is known: rough estimate, survey, or final measurement.
  • Quote reference/version and terms are saved.
  • Follow-up date is set if customer does not reply.
  • Customer acceptance is recorded in writing before sold/contract setup.
Gate: move forward only when exact quote version, customer approval, and any bank/insurance dependency are known.
Stage 4 - Contract + DepositAcceptance to payment control

Accepted work must become a contract/work order with the correct quote version. Deposit invoice, payment link, bank-reference instruction, and reconciliation must tie to the same job.

  1. Confirm acceptance. Verify the customer accepted the correct quote version.
  2. Create or update contract. Keep lead, quote, and contract linked.
  3. Raise deposit invoice. Include invoice number, quote reference, payment link, and bank-reference instruction.
  4. Match payment. Connect provider event or bank transfer to invoice and contract.
  5. Trigger next stage. Usually final measurement, specification lock, or installation scheduling - not automatic ordering.
Gate: move forward only when the contract exists, scope matches the approved quote, and payment is received/reconciled if required.
Stage 5 - Pre-Order ControlThe manufacture-readiness lock

This is the most important control in the company. The job is not order-ready until final measurement and specification details are locked and variations are cleared.

Spec lock

Dimensions, style, material, finish, components, accessories, trims, thresholds, and access requirements.

Risk lock

Customer-supplied items, price increases, lead-time changes, split invoices, technical conflicts, and measurement discrepancies.

Manufacture-readiness checks

  • Final manufacture dimensions are known or explicitly not required.
  • Quote version, contract scope, and customer decisions still match.
  • Variation price, credit, refund, or additional invoice is resolved.
  • Supplier lead-time or price risk has been checked where relevant.
  • Customer-supplied accessories or special requirements are specified.
  • Installer/access constraints are visible before ordering.
Gate: move forward only when payment control is complete, final measure/spec are complete where required, and any variation has written approval and finance action.
Stage 6 - Supplier OrderOrder placed to acknowledged

Supplier ordering is the irreversible/costly fulfilment event. The order must be traceable and every supplier response must feed the job record.

  • Supplier selected and order drafted from locked specification.
  • Purchase/order approval is clear before the order is placed.
  • Supplier order placed and reference recorded.
  • Order acknowledgement saved.
  • Price, lead time, expected delivery, and supplier queries are logged.
  • Delivery follow-up task is created.

Stage 8 - InstallationConfirmed date to practical completion

Installation is where office promises meet the site. Confirming installation requires material confidence, installer availability, customer availability, access, and prep instructions.

Customer prep checklist

  • Remove ornaments, photos, fragile items, curtains, and blinds near work areas where possible.
  • Move small furniture; clarify responsibility for heavy or fragile items.
  • Warn about noise, tools, dust sheets, carpet protection, and temporary exposure to cold/drafts.
  • Keep children, pets, and vulnerable occupants away from work zones.
  • Confirm access, parking, power, alarms, locks, sensors, and special instructions.
Gate: move to finance only when completion status, snags, remedials, photos/notes, customer update, and invoice readiness are clear.
Stage 9 - Final FinanceFinal invoice to paid-in-full

Final finance reconciles the job. It is not just sending an invoice; it is checking completion status, variation history, credits/refunds, and payment matching.

  • Final invoice references correct quote/contract.
  • Outstanding amount reflects variations, credits, refunds, or extras.
  • Payment link and bank-reference instruction are included.
  • Payment is matched against invoice and contract.
  • Any disputed, split, missing, or failed payment is tracked as an accounts action.
Gate: move to close only when final payment is reconciled or a named exception remains open with owner and date.
Stage 10 - Warranty / CloseSupport, review, and archive

Closure is not silence. Closure means the customer has been supported, any remedial issue has a home, warranty/guarantee information is handled where needed, and the CRM state matches reality.

  • Review request sent only when customer sentiment supports it.
  • Warranty/guarantee information sent or stored if required.
  • Remedials closed or split into a separate active issue.
  • Manufacturer/supplier part chases closed or assigned.
  • CRM contract status updated to reflect closure.

The pipeline is the spine. These are the organs: finance, communications, exceptions, accounts, and daily management.

Finance Control

A payment notification must be matched before it changes the job stage.

Exception Playbooks

Where Margin, Trust, and Time Get Lost

Most expensive mistakes are not normal pipeline events. They are exceptions handled as if they were normal.

Customer changes scope after payment

  1. Pause ordering if possible. Do not keep moving while the agreed scope is unstable.
  2. Find the order/manufacture status. Not placed, placed, acknowledged, manufactured, or delivered are different realities.
  3. Get the requested change in writing. Vague phone changes create dispute risk.
  4. Ask technical owner for feasibility. The office should not invent feasibility.
  5. Calculate price and lead-time impact. Higher price, lower price, no change, delay, or impossible.
  6. Issue variation/revised quote. Preserve original quote and invoice history.
  7. Handle money. Additional invoice, credit note, refund, offset, or no-balance note.
  8. Update job record. Supplier, installer, customer, and accounts need the same truth.

Payment not matched

Check provider status, bank feed, amount, invoice number, customer reference, payment time/date, and payer identity. Confirm only once matched.

Customer-supplied accessory

Require make/model/specification before ordering. Explain that wrong accessory requirements can create remake, delay, and warranty ambiguity.

Customer asks for faster installation

Check supplier lead time, delivery confidence, installer capacity, and whether the current product/order status permits an earlier date. Never promise speed before checking the constraint layer.

Remedial or warranty issue

Acknowledge quickly, request photos/video, link original job, assign technical/install owner, classify warranty vs chargeable vs supplier/manufacturer issue, and keep the customer updated.

Supplier delay or manufacturing uncertainty

Separate what is known from what is hoped. Record supplier reference, acknowledgement state, last chase date, promised update date, affected install date, customer promise already made, and whether a backup product/date/installer plan exists.

Implementation

How To Turn This Into Operating Reality

The manual becomes useful only when it is reduced into fields, gates, templates, and daily rituals.

  1. Define required CRM fields. Primary stage, stacked statuses, owner, next action, quote version, deposit status, measurement confidence, supplier order ref, delivery date, install date, remedial flag.
  2. Separate the five entities. Lead, contract, install, remedial, and finance can share one CRM contract, but the review board must keep their truths apart.
  3. Create stage gate checklists. Put each gate in the stage where the work happens, not in a separate handbook no one opens.
  4. Add a supplier subpipeline. Track PO created, approved, placed, acknowledged, manufacturing, delivery confirmed, delivered, and checked.
  5. Add a remedial lifecycle. Track issue logged, classified, evidence requested, parts/supplier answer, return visit, resolved, and closed.
  6. Map templates to stages. One folder or CRM template library should match lead, survey, quote, deposit, final measure, supplier risk, install, finance, and warranty.
  7. Build the daily review board. Filter by overdue, due today, waiting customer, waiting supplier, waiting accounts, and install risk.
  8. Assign owners by decision type. Office, technical, accounts, installer, or management approval.
  9. Run a two-week correction loop. Every time a job gets stuck, add the missing field, checklist item, or template phrase.
  10. Audit closed jobs monthly. Check quote version, payment matching, final measure, supplier reference, install notes, final payment, and remedial closure.
