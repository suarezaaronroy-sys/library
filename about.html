01 · The thesis
Most operational breakdowns are not effort problems. They are structural.

This site began because I kept needing my own notes. Payment processing taught me that undocumented knowledge walks out the door with people. CRM work taught me that a system nobody can explain is not much of a system.

So I write the manual for everything: first as working reference, then as a public artifact. The library doubles as a portfolio, but that is the side effect. Its primary function is still the same one it had on day one. It is where I look things up.

I work across roles because real operations do not respect job-title borders. A handoff can begin in marketing, break in a CRM, surface in payments, and finally become a management problem. The useful unit of analysis is the whole path.

02 · Working method

From field reality to something that lasts.

The software matters. The sequence matters more.

01 Observe

A survey begins before the tool opens. I look for handoffs, workarounds, exceptions, and what experienced people notice without writing down.

02 Model

I reconstruct the mechanism: dependencies, ownership, incentives, failure modes, and the information each decision actually needs.

03 Build

Then the structure becomes fields, automations, safeguards, interfaces, fallback logic, and a path people can follow under pressure.

04 Write

The handoff is not finished until the next person can understand, verify, repair, and extend what was built.

Documentation is not the receipt for the system. It is one of its working parts.

04 · The through-line

Competence over appearances. Mechanisms over theatre.

My first question is rarely “what should I do?” It is “how does this work?” Once the mechanism is clear, the fix usually becomes reconstructable instead of mysterious.

That is why the same approach keeps working across CRMs, payment processing, marketing engines, organizational design, and the occasional motorcycle. They are all systems of forces, tolerances, dependencies, and consequences.

Off the clock, I tend toward machines with mechanical honesty and fiction about hidden infrastructure, reality, and control. The subjects change. The curiosity does not.
